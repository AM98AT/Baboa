# **Educational Caregiver Guide: Navigating Geriatric Nephrology and Gastrointestinal Oncology**

Managing the care of an older adult facing the dual challenges of advanced age and colon cancer is a profoundly complex and often overwhelming endeavor. When an 86-year-old patient with a gastrointestinal malignancy also begins to experience fluctuating kidney function and severe electrolyte imbalances, family caregivers are frequently thrust into a highly technical, delicate, and high-stakes medical environment. The intersection of geriatric nephrology (the study of aging kidneys) and gastrointestinal oncology requires an intricate balancing act. Interventions that may benefit the cancer treatment can sometimes harm the kidneys, and treatments meant to support the kidneys can sometimes complicate overall oncology care and quality of life1.  
This comprehensive, in-depth educational guide is designed to empower you, the family caregiver. It translates complex clinical data into actionable, plain-language insights. Specifically, this guide analyzes a highly fragile clinical scenario: an 86-year-old man with colon cancer whose serum creatinine is fluctuating between 2.09 and 2.78 (currently at 2.20), whose serum urea nitrogen experienced a sudden, dangerous spike from 31 to 92.1 (currently settling at 74.6), whose sodium levels are consistently low between 126.9 and 130.7, and whose potassium remains stable at 4.39.  
By deeply understanding the physiological mechanisms behind these laboratory numbers, you can better anticipate medical complications, communicate far more effectively with oncology and nephrology teams, and provide a safe, compassionate, and highly supportive environment for your loved one.

## **1\. Translating the Clinical Data: Understanding Kidney Function and Electrolytes**

To provide effective daily care, the caregiver must first learn to understand the language of the laboratory. Blood tests act as a direct window into the patient's internal environment, specifically highlighting how well the kidneys are filtering waste and maintaining chemical equilibrium3. In an 86-year-old body, organ systems possess significantly less reserve capacity to handle physical stress, making these numbers highly sensitive indicators of overall health and stability.  
The kidneys are sophisticated filtration plants. They are responsible for removing waste, balancing electrolytes, producing hormones that regulate blood pressure (renin), and signaling the bone marrow to produce red blood cells (erythropoietin)1. When they fail, the entire body is impacted.

### **Decoding Serum Creatinine**

Creatinine is a natural, continuous waste product generated by the normal wear and tear of muscle tissue5. Under healthy conditions, the kidneys continuously filter creatinine out of the bloodstream and excrete it into the urine6.

* **Normal Ranges:** For adult males, a normal serum creatinine level generally sits between 0.7 and 1.2 milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL)7.  
* **The Patient's Scenario:** In this specific clinical profile, the serum creatinine has fluctuated between 2.09 and 2.78, currently resting at 2.20.  
* **The Deeper Implication for an 86-Year-Old:** A creatinine level consistently above 2.0 mg/dL indicates that the kidneys are working at a drastically reduced capacity1. However, the context of the patient's age and cancer is vital. Creatinine production is tied to muscle mass7. An 86-year-old man fighting colon cancer likely has age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia) and cancer-related muscle wasting (cachexia)11. Because he has very little muscle mass producing creatinine, a reading of 2.20 is actually far more alarming than it would be in a younger, muscular man. It means the kidney filtration rate is exceptionally low. The fluctuation between 2.09 and 2.78 indicates a highly fragile state where the kidneys are vulnerable to daily changes in hydration, blood pressure, or medication toxicity9.

### **Decoding Serum Urea (Blood Urea Nitrogen \- BUN)**

Urea, often measured in clinical labs as Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN), is a waste product formed in the liver when the body breaks down proteins12. Like creatinine, it relies entirely on the kidneys for excretion.

* **Normal Ranges:** Normal BUN levels typically range from 10 to 20 mg/dL14.  
* **The Patient's Scenario:** The patient’s urea spiked dramatically from an already elevated 31 to a severe 92.1, before dropping back to 74.6.  
* **The Deeper Implication of the Spike:** A massive jump in urea indicates an acute (sudden) crisis superimposed on chronic kidney strain1. While creatinine measures pure filtration capability, urea is highly sensitive to hydration levels and blood flow. A sudden spike to 92.1 strongly suggests two major possibilities in a colon cancer patient:  
  1. **Severe Dehydration:** The body did not have enough fluid to push the urea through the kidneys, causing it to back up into the blood.  
  2. **Gastrointestinal Bleeding:** Colon cancer can cause occult (hidden) bleeding into the digestive tract. When blood is digested in the stomach and intestines, the body absorbs the protein from the blood, and the liver converts that massive protein load into urea, causing a dramatic spike15.  
* The current reduction to 74.6 shows improvement, likely due to medical intervention or rehydration, but it remains dangerously high. This indicates significant uremia (the presence of toxins in the blood) that can cause profound physical symptoms1.

### **Distinguishing Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) vs. Chronic Kidney Strain**

The data presents a classic, complex picture of Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) occurring on top of underlying Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). Understanding the difference helps caregivers manage expectations and daily care.

* **Chronic Kidney Strain (CKD):** The baseline creatinine hovering around 2.0 represents chronic, long-term wear and tear9. The kidneys have sustained permanent damage, likely exacerbated by a lifetime of use (86 years), potential blood pressure fluctuations, and the physical toll of colon cancer and its treatments16. This chronic baseline is largely irreversible.  
* **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI):** The sudden, violent spike in urea to 92.1 and the fluctuations in creatinine represent an acute injury—a sudden, temporary worsening of function1. AKI is often triggered by dehydration, an infection, a toxic medication, or a drop in blood pressure. Caregivers must recognize that while the underlying CKD is permanent, the acute spikes (AKI) can often be managed, reversed, or prevented with careful daily fluid and medical management1.

### **Understanding Sodium (Na) and Potassium (K)**

Electrolytes are minerals that carry an electric charge. They are the chemical messengers of the body, regulating nerve impulses, muscle contractions, heartbeat, and hydration levels3.

* **Sodium (Na):** Normal sodium ranges from 135 to 145 mEq/L. The patient’s levels are consistently low, ranging between 126.9 and 130.7. This condition is known as **hyponatremia**17. It means the blood is too dilute. Sodium regulates water movement in the body; when blood sodium is low, water shifts into the body's cells, causing them to swell. If brain cells swell, it leads to severe neurological consequences18.  
* **Potassium (K):** Normal potassium ranges from 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L. The patient’s potassium is remarkably stable at 4.39. This is excellent and highly reassuring news. The kidneys are primarily responsible for clearing excess potassium; when kidneys fail, potassium builds up, which can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias20. The stable potassium indicates that, despite the high urea and creatinine, the remaining functional parts of the kidneys are still managing this critical heart-regulating electrolyte effectively.

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Serum Creatinine** | 0.7 \- 1.2 mg/dL | 2.20 (Range: 2.09 \- 2.78) | Chronic kidney strain. Given the patient's age and likely low muscle mass, a 2.20 indicates significantly compromised filtration capacity. |
| **Serum Urea (BUN)** | 10 \- 20 mg/dL | 74.6 (Spiked to 92.1) | Severe buildup of protein waste. The past spike indicates severe dehydration, acute kidney injury, or potential GI bleeding. |
| **Sodium (Na)** | 135 \- 145 mEq/L | 126.9 \- 130.7 | Hyponatremia. The blood is dangerously dilute, carrying high risks of cellular swelling, neurological confusion, and falls. |
| **Potassium (K)** | 3.5 \- 5.0 mEq/L | 4.39 (Stable) | Normal. A positive sign that the kidneys are still successfully maintaining cardiac-safe potassium levels. |

## **2\. The Hydration Dilemma: Balancing Hyponatremia and Kidney Strain**

One of the most complex, anxiety-inducing, and counter-intuitive tasks for the caregiver in this specific scenario is managing the patient's daily hydration. The clinical data presents a profound physiological contradiction that makes standard, well-meaning advice (such as "drink eight glasses of water a day") not just incorrect, but potentially life-threatening.

### **The Physiological Paradox**

To understand the caregiver's dilemma, we must look at the conflicting signals the body is sending:

1. **The High Urea (The Dehydration Signal):** A high urea level (BUN of 74.6) typically signals that the body is "dry." In a healthy person, the response to high urea is to drink copious amounts of water to help the kidneys flush out the accumulated toxins.  
2. **The Low Sodium (The Over-dilution Signal):** A low sodium level (hyponatremia) indicates that there is already relatively too much free water in the blood compared to salt17. Furthermore, stressed, aging kidneys (indicated by the high creatinine) lose their ability to quickly excrete excess water22.

### **The Severe Danger of Plain Water (Water Intoxication)**

When a patient has chronic hyponatremia (sodium in the 126-130 range), forcing them to drink plain water is highly dangerous. Plain water contains virtually no electrolytes23. When plain water enters a bloodstream that is already starved of sodium, it further dilutes the blood, dropping the sodium levels even lower.  
Through the biological process of osmosis, water always moves from areas of low salt concentration to areas of high salt concentration. If the blood becomes too dilute from drinking plain water, that water will rapidly shift out of the bloodstream and into the more concentrated cells of the body, causing them to swell.  
If this swelling occurs in the brain, it leads to a condition called hyponatremic encephalopathy. The rigid skull cannot accommodate the swelling brain tissue. This can result in severe lethargy, profound delirium and confusion, intractable hiccups, muscle weakness, and in extreme cases, seizures, coma, and death18.

### **Caregiver Strategies for the Hydration Dilemma**

To safely navigate this delicate dilemma, the caregiver must act as a strict manager of fluid intake, following explicit, medically supervised protocols:

* **Avoid Unrestricted Plain Water:** You must not leave large pitchers of plain water at the bedside for unrestricted, unmonitored consumption23. While the patient may feel thirsty due to dry mouth (a common side effect of age, medications, and uremia), giving them unlimited plain water will worsen the hyponatremia.  
* **Utilize Electrolyte Solutions:** If the oncology and nephrology medical teams permit oral fluid intake, it should frequently be in the form of specialized oral rehydration solutions, broths, or fluids that contain a safe balance of sodium and other electrolytes, rather than plain water25. Always ask the doctor which specific electrolyte drinks are safe, as some sports drinks contain too much sugar or inappropriate levels of potassium.  
* **Adhere to Prescribed Fluid Restrictions:** In many cases of chronic kidney disease coupled with hyponatremia, the physician will prescribe a strict daily fluid restriction (for example, limiting total fluid to 1.2 or 1.5 liters per day)26. The caregiver must rigorously measure all fluids consumed. It is critical to remember that "fluids" include not just water and juice, but also soups, broths, gelatin, ice cream, and ice chips25.  
* **Manage Thirst Safely:** Thirst can be a source of immense suffering for the patient. If the patient is on a fluid restriction but complains of severe dry mouth, caregivers can offer relief without breaking fluid limits by providing hard, sugar-free sour candies to stimulate saliva, using specialized dry-mouth swabs, applying lip balms to prevent cracking, or offering small, measured ice chips (which melt down to very small volumes of water)25.  
* **Monitor for Fluid Overload:** Because the kidneys are struggling to filter (Creatinine \> 2.0), the patient is at high risk of retaining fluid. This excess fluid often does not stay in the blood; gravity pulls it down, causing it to leak into tissues. This creates edema (swelling) in the legs, ankles, and abdomen, or dangerously, it can back up into the lungs (pulmonary edema)22.

## **3\. Medication Warnings: Protecting Fragile Kidneys**

For an 86-year-old with colon cancer and fragile, declining kidneys, medication management is an absolute minefield. The aging liver and kidneys clear medications much more slowly than younger organs. Many common, over-the-counter drugs that seem perfectly harmless—and that the patient may have taken safely for decades—can now precipitate sudden, irreversible kidney failure1. The caregiver must act as the primary gatekeeper, ensuring no nephrotoxic (kidney-damaging) substances ever reach the patient without explicit doctor approval.

### **The Extreme Danger of NSAIDs (Painkillers)**

Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs) are the single most common culprits of preventable acute kidney injury in older adults28. Common examples include:

* Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)  
* Naproxen (Aleve, Naprosyn)  
* Celecoxib (Celebrex)  
* Aspirin (in high pain-relief doses; note that low-dose 81mg cardiac aspirin is usually managed safely by a cardiologist, but must still be monitored)30

**The Mechanism of Harm:** The kidneys rely on a robust, high-pressure blood supply to filter waste. To maintain this vital blood flow, the kidneys produce local chemicals called prostaglandins, which act to keep the blood vessels leading into the kidneys wide open. NSAIDs relieve pain by blocking the exact enzymes that create prostaglandins throughout the body31. When an older adult with already struggling kidneys takes an NSAID, the blood vessels entering the kidneys immediately constrict, drastically dropping the blood supply. This starves the delicate kidney tissue of oxygen, rapidly accelerating Acute Kidney Injury and driving creatinine and urea levels even higher20.  
Furthermore, NSAIDs significantly increase the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding20. For a patient with colon cancer, introducing a medication that thins the blood and damages the stomach lining is exceptionally dangerous and can lead to severe hemorrhage.

* **Caregiver Action:** The caregiver must absolutely ban all over-the-counter NSAIDs from the home unless explicitly prescribed by the nephrologist. For mild pain, acetaminophen (Tylenol) is generally metabolized by the liver rather than the kidneys and is often considered the safer alternative. However, the total daily dose must still be strictly regulated by a physician to prevent liver damage, especially if the patient is eating poorly30.

### **Antibiotics, Cold Medicines, and Contrast Dyes**

* **Antibiotics:** Infections are common in older adults fighting cancer. However, many antibiotics are cleared entirely by the kidneys. If a urinary tract infection or pneumonia arises, the caregiver must explicitly remind the prescribing doctor (especially urgent care doctors who may not know the patient's history) of the patient's elevated creatinine (2.20). The antibiotic dosage must be "renally adjusted." Giving a standard adult dose of renally-cleared antibiotics to a patient with compromised kidneys will lead to massive toxic buildup in the bloodstream, potentially causing hearing loss, seizures, or further kidney failure.  
* **Cold and Sinus Medications:** Over-the-counter decongestants containing pseudoephedrine constrict blood vessels to clear the nose. Unfortunately, they also constrict blood vessels systemically, raising blood pressure and reducing blood flow to the kidneys32. These must be avoided.  
* **Contrast Dyes for Imaging:** As a colon cancer patient, the individual will likely undergo periodic CT scans or MRIs to monitor the malignancy and check for metastasis. Intravenous contrast dye used in many of these scans is highly toxic to the kidneys (a condition known as contrast-induced nephropathy)15. The caregiver must ensure the oncology team is acutely aware of the current kidney panels prior to any imaging. The oncologists may opt for non-contrast scans, use different imaging modalities, or provide specialized protective intravenous fluids before and after the scan to flush the dye safely through the fragile kidneys.

### **Unverified Over-the-Counter Supplements**

Many herbal supplements, teas, and high-dose vitamins can severely interfere with kidney function or interact negatively with cancer treatments. Products containing high levels of vitamin C can cause kidney stones, while certain herbal teas and undocumented supplements can strain renal filtration or alter the blood levels of chemotherapy drugs. The caregiver should place every single pill, capsule, or tea in a bag and clear it directly with the medical team.

| Medication Category | Examples to Avoid/Monitor | Reason for Caution in this Patient |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **NSAIDs** | Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Celecoxib, High-dose Aspirin | Constricts kidney blood vessels causing Acute Kidney Injury; increases risk of fatal GI bleeding20. |
| **Antibiotics** | Various (depends on prescription) | Must be "renally dosed." Standard doses will build up to toxic levels due to poor kidney clearance. |
| **Decongestants** | Pseudoephedrine | Raises blood pressure; constricts blood flow to the kidneys33. |
| **Imaging Dyes** | IV Contrast for CT Scans | Highly toxic to struggling kidneys (contrast-induced nephropathy)15. |
| **Supplements** | High-dose vitamins, herbal teas | Can cause kidney stones or interact unpredictably with oncology medications. |

## **4\. What to Do: Daily Tracking, Diet, and Caregiver Protocols**

Active, meticulous observation is the most powerful tool a caregiver possesses. You are the eyes and ears of the medical team. By tracking specific metrics daily, the caregiver can identify a brewing kidney crisis days before it escalates into a hospital emergency.

### **Tracking Fluid Intake and Output (I\&Os)**

Because the patient's kidneys are functioning poorly, what goes into the body must be carefully compared to what comes out34. This balance is the clearest indicator of whether the kidneys are keeping up with the body's demands.

* **Intake Tracking:** The caregiver should keep a dedicated notebook or use an app to maintain a written log of every milliliter of fluid the patient consumes over 24 hours. This includes water, tea, broth, oral medications taken with water, and foods that melt at room temperature (like gelatin or ice cream)25.  
* **Output Tracking (Urine):** The caregiver must monitor urine volume. To do this accurately, the medical supply store or hospital can provide a "hat"—a plastic measuring receptacle that fits under the toilet seat6. Male patients can also use a handheld graduated urinal. A sudden decrease in urination (oliguria) is a glaring red flag that the kidneys are shutting down, holding onto fluid, or that a tumor or enlarged prostate is blocking the urinary tract1.  
* **Observing Urine Appearance:** The color and consistency of the urine provide vital clues about internal health36.  
  * *Dark, amber, or tea-colored urine* indicates severe dehydration; the kidneys are desperately holding onto water and only excreting concentrated waste.  
  * *Frothy or excessively bubbly urine* can indicate protein spilling through damaged kidney filters into the urine.  
  * *Red, pink, or cola-colored urine* (hematuria) requires immediate medical attention, as it may signify bleeding from the kidneys, bladder, or complications related to the cancer or blood-thinning medications36.

### **Monitoring Fluid Retention (Edema)**

When the kidneys fail to excrete the fluid the patient drinks, gravity pulls the excess water to the lowest points of the body, or it settles in the abdomen.

* **Strict Daily Weighing:** This is non-negotiable. The caregiver should weigh the patient every single morning. The rules for accuracy are strict: weigh the patient at the exact same time every day, using the same scale, wearing the same amount of clothing, after they use the restroom to empty their bladder, but before they eat breakfast1. A sudden weight gain of 2 to 3 pounds in a single 24-hour period, or 5 pounds in a week, is almost entirely fluid retention, not fat or muscle gain. This sudden fluid accumulation must be reported immediately to the doctor22.  
* **Physical Checks for Edema:** The caregiver should gently press a thumb into the skin over the patient's shin, ankle, or top of the foot for five seconds. If the thumb leaves an indentation (a "pit") that takes time to slowly fill back in, this is called pitting edema. This is a classic, undeniable sign of fluid overload resulting from kidney strain and requires medical review22.

### **Managing Diet and Nutrition in GI Oncology and Nephrology**

Diet plays a crucial role in managing both uremia (toxin buildup) and colon cancer recovery. The dietary requirements for these two conditions sometimes clash, requiring careful meal planning11.

* **Navigating the Sodium Paradox:** The paradox of hyponatremia means the patient has low blood sodium. However, dietary sodium must still be managed strictly according to the physician's orders. If the physician has diagnosed fluid overload, they may still restrict dietary sodium (to prevent further water retention and swelling) despite the low blood levels22.  
  * If a low-sodium diet is ordered, caregivers must avoid processed foods, canned soups, deli meats, and frozen dinners. Instead of table salt, flavor foods with lemon juice, balsamic vinegar, garlic, onion powder, and safe herb blends26.  
  * *Crucial Warning:* Never use "salt substitutes" (like NoSalt) without doctor approval. Most salt substitutes replace sodium with potassium. Since kidney patients are at high risk for fatal potassium buildup, these substitutes can be deadly27.  
* **Managing Protein Intake:** Because urea (BUN) is a direct byproduct of protein breakdown, feeding an 86-year-old with a BUN of 74.6 a diet excessively high in heavy red meats can rapidly worsen uremic toxicity. The medical team and a registered oncology dietitian will usually recommend high-quality, easily digestible proteins in highly measured amounts (such as eggs, poultry, or fish) to maintain muscle mass without overloading the kidney's waste processing systems11.  
* **Overcoming Taste Changes:** High urea levels and cancer treatments often cause food to taste like cardboard or metal. To combat this, serve foods cold or at room temperature (which decreases strong smells that cause nausea), use plastic silverware if food tastes metallic, and experiment with tart flavors like cranberry or lemon to cut through the metallic taste of uremia11.

## **5\. Physical & Mental Signs: Recognizing Toxicity and Electrolyte Imbalances**

When the kidneys fail to clear toxins (leading to high urea and creatinine) and the blood becomes too dilute (low sodium), the brain and body suffer profoundly. The caregiver must be hyper-vigilant for the subtle physical and mental changes that precede a severe medical crisis. Identifying these symptoms early allows for outpatient adjustments before a traumatic hospital admission is required.

### **Signs of Uremic Toxicity (High Urea)**

As urea builds up in the blood (approaching the 70-90+ mg/dL range), it acts as a systemic poison, affecting virtually every organ system1.

* **Gastrointestinal Distress:** The patient may experience severe, unrelenting nausea, vomiting, and a profound loss of appetite. They may complain of a metallic taste in their mouth, a classic sign of uremia altering the taste buds and saliva11.  
* **Skin Changes and Pruritus:** Uremic toxins, particularly calcium and phosphorus imbalances, can accumulate in the skin. This causes intense, unrelenting, deep-tissue itching (pruritus) without any visible rash. Patients may scratch themselves until they bleed40.  
* **Profound Fatigue:** The patient will experience a bone-deep, crushing lethargy. This is not normal tiredness; it is a metabolic exhaustion that does not improve with sleep or rest1.

### **Signs of Hyponatremia (Low Sodium)**

Because sodium regulates the electrical signals of the brain and the firing of muscles, low sodium (126-130 mEq/L) manifests primarily as neurological and muscular dysfunction18.

* **Early Physical Signs:** The caregiver may notice the patient developing intractable, uncontrollable hiccups, subtle muscle twitching (especially in the face or hands), or a new clumsiness and loss of balance. This balance issue drastically raises the risk of dangerous falls in the home19.  
* **Mental Confusion and Delirium (The Greatest Danger):** This is the most critical sign to watch for. While an 86-year-old may have a degree of baseline age-related cognitive decline, hyponatremia causes **delirium**—a sudden, acute state of severe confusion. The patient may forget where they are, fail to recognize family members, experience sudden mood swings (ranging from unrousably sleepy to violently agitated), or have vivid visual hallucinations41.

### **Caregiver Response to Confusion and Delirium**

If the patient becomes confused or delirious due to these metabolic imbalances, it is highly distressing for the family. The caregiver must remain calm and implement safety protocols:

* **Ensure Safety:** Make the environment safe. Remove tripping hazards, ensure the room is well-lit during the day, and use a nightlight to prevent disorientation in the dark41.  
* **Reorientation:** Speak in calm, simple, reassuring sentences. Frequently reorient the patient to the time and place. Place a large-print clock and calendar in their line of sight41.  
* **Do Not Argue:** Never argue with hallucinations or delusions. It causes severe distress. Instead, offer comfort, acknowledge their feelings, and immediately contact the medical team. Acute confusion signifies that the brain is currently swelling or being poisoned by toxins, requiring medical intervention41.  
* **Seizure Precautions:** Because extremely low sodium can trigger seizures, ensure the patient is not left alone in high-risk areas (like the bathtub or standing near stairs)24.

## **6\. Emergency Red Flags: When to Seek Immediate Help**

Despite the most meticulous and loving caregiver efforts, an 86-year-old body fighting colon cancer and kidney disease is inherently fragile. The system may reach a breaking point where the kidneys fail completely or electrolytes shift into a lethal range. Knowing exactly when to bypass outpatient clinics and call for immediate emergency medical intervention is life-saving.  
The caregiver must constantly watch for the following absolute medical emergencies:

1. **Anuria (Complete Cessation of Urine):** If the patient goes 8 to 12 hours without producing any urine, or if the daily urine output drops to a mere trickle despite fluid intake, the kidneys may have stopped filtering entirely, or a tumor or enlarged prostate may be physically blocking the urinary tract. This requires immediate emergency intervention to drain the bladder or start emergency dialysis22.  
2. **Pulmonary Edema (Fluid in the Lungs):** If the kidneys stop making urine, the fluid the patient drinks has nowhere to go. It backs up into the vascular system and eventually overflows into the lungs. Symptoms include a sudden, severe inability to breathe, a wet, rattling sound in the chest, the inability to lie flat without gasping for air, or coughing up pink, frothy sputum. **This is a life-threatening medical emergency. The patient is essentially drowning in their own fluids**22.  
3. **Hyponatremic Crisis (Seizures and Coma):** If sodium levels drop too fast or fall significantly below the 120s, brain swelling becomes critical. This can trigger seizures (uncontrolled physical convulsions, blank staring spells, or sudden loss of bowel/bladder control) or cause the patient to slip into an unrousable state of coma18.

### **Specific Emergency Instructions for Caregivers in Duhok, Iraq**

If any of the above emergency red flags are observed—no urine output, severe shortness of breath, sudden delirium, or seizures—the caregiver must not wait to see if the symptoms pass. Action must be swift and decisive:

* **Call for an Ambulance immediately by dialing 122\.**  
* **Seek immediate emergency medical care at:**  
  * **Azadi Teaching Hospital**  
  * **Duhok Emergency and Trauma Teaching Hospital**

**What to tell the emergency responders:** When interacting with the emergency dispatchers, ambulance medics, or triage nurses at the hospital, the caregiver must immediately present the clinical data clearly and concisely. State:  
*"The patient is an 86-year-old male with colon cancer. He has a known history of severe kidney strain with high creatinine (over 2.0), recent extreme urea spikes, and chronic low sodium (hyponatremia)."*  
Providing this precise information is vital. It allows the trauma and emergency teams to immediately check electrolyte panels and avoid standard treatments (like rapid administration of normal saline IV fluids) that could inadvertently worsen the kidney failure or cause fatal brain complications due to rapid sodium shifts.
